Five Bullet Friday — The Halfway Mark

June 26

We're past the solstice. The year's halfway done. The heat is the kind that doesn't cool off at night anymore, and your body has been running the summer program for a solid month. Five things for the back half.

1. A move — the doorway pec stretch

If you've been spending your evenings on the couch after long days in the heat, your chest is probably locked short. The pecs pull your shoulders forward, your upper back rounds to compensate, and by Thursday you've got that tight band between the shoulder blades that nothing seems to touch. The problem isn't in your back. It's in the front.

Stand in a doorway. Place your forearms on either side of the frame, elbows at shoulder height, palms flat. Step one foot forward until you feel the stretch across your chest and the front of your shoulders. Hold for 30 seconds. Then move your elbows up about four inches — this shifts the stretch to the upper pecs and the area near your collarbone. Hold another 30 seconds.

Two positions, one minute total. Do it every time you walk through that doorway and the habit builds itself.

2. A tool — a basic foam roller ($15-20)

We've covered the lacrosse ball, the golf ball, and the frozen water bottle. A foam roller is the next step up — and for your mid-back and thoracic spine, it's better than any of them.

Lie on it lengthwise (along your spine, not across it) with your arms out to the sides, palms up. Let gravity open your chest. Stay there for two minutes. That's it. No rolling, no effort — just let your body drape over it. This reverses the forward-shoulder pattern that summer activities lock in: mowing, driving, carrying coolers, hunching over a grill.

The smooth, medium-density ones are fine. Skip the textured or extra-firm versions unless you already know you want them. Any sporting goods store carries them. Around $15 at most places, and they last for years.

3. A nugget — Matthew Walker on sleep and muscle recovery

Matthew Walker — neuroscientist at UC Berkeley and author of Why We Sleep — has a finding that matters more in June than any other month: deep sleep is when your body releases the majority of its growth hormone, which is what actually repairs muscle tissue. Cut your deep sleep short and the repair doesn't happen. The soreness doesn't clear. The tightness carries over to the next day, and the next.

The problem in late June is exactly what you'd expect. Longer daylight hours push bedtimes later. Heat disrupts sleep cycles. The body's doing more and recovering less. If you've noticed that things are taking longer to bounce back this month, your sleep is the first place to look — before the stretches, before the table, before anything.

4. A practice — 90 seconds of humming

This one sounds odd. It works.

Take a slow breath in through your nose. On the exhale, hum — mouth closed, steady tone, let the vibration fill your chest and throat. One long hum per exhale. Six to eight rounds. Takes about 90 seconds.

Humming stimulates the vagus nerve — the long nerve that runs from your brainstem to your gut and controls your parasympathetic response. Activating it tells your nervous system to downshift. Heart rate drops, muscle tension eases, the mental chatter quiets. It's the same mechanism behind why chanting works in every contemplative tradition, stripped down to the simplest possible version. No app, no technique to learn. Just hum.

5. A seasonal note

Late June in Missouri. The heat index has been serious, the Fourth of July weekend is next week, and most people I see right now are carrying about a month of accumulated summer strain — yard work, travel, projects, heat, not enough sleep.

This is the time of year when small issues stop being small. The hip that's been nagging since Memorial Day. The shoulder that only hurts when you reach overhead. The low back that tightens every morning. A month of ignoring it turns a one-session fix into a three-session project.

If something's been building, this is a good week to come in — before the holiday weekend adds another layer. The table's open Monday, Wednesday, and Friday.
