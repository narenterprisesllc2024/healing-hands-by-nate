A 2-minute back opener, a $3 DIY heat pack, and what to ask before you book anywhere.

Five Bullet Friday · June 5

The Early Summer Edition

First real heat week in Missouri. The gardens are producing, the AC is earning its keep, and your body is adjusting to a different kind of demand. Five things worth knowing this week.

1. A move — the open-book stretch for a locked-up mid-back

If you've been bent over garden beds, a steering wheel, or a desk all week, your thoracic spine is probably stuck in flexion. This takes two minutes and usually unlocks something you didn't realize was locked.

Lie on your side, knees stacked and bent to 90 degrees. Extend both arms straight out in front of you, palms together. Slowly open the top arm like you're opening a book — reach it up and over to the other side, following it with your eyes. Let your chest rotate but keep your knees stacked. When you feel the stretch across your chest and mid-back, hold for 3-5 slow breaths. Return slowly. Five reps per side.

Most people hear an audible pop on the first rep. That's normal and usually means the joint was asking for it.

2. A tool — a DIY rice sock ($3 and 10 minutes)

Fill a tube sock with 2-3 cups of uncooked rice. Tie the end. Microwave for 90 seconds. You now own a moldable heat pack that conforms to your neck, low back, or shoulder better than any rigid heating pad on the shelf.

Rice holds heat for 20-30 minutes. It costs almost nothing. And it's the tool I recommend more than anything else to clients dealing with chronic tightness — especially now that summer AC is about to make every muscle in your body want to clench up overnight. The hot-day, cold-house cycle is real, and a rice sock at bedtime breaks it.

3. A nugget — you're dehydrated before you feel thirsty

The American College of Sports Medicine has been saying this for decades: even mild dehydration — as little as 2% body weight loss — measurably increases muscle stiffness and reduces range of motion. The problem is that thirst lags behind the deficit. By the time you want water, your tissues have already tightened.

I see it on the table every June. The clients who show up tightest are almost always the ones who worked outside for two hours before they remembered to drink anything. Front-load water before you go out. Sixteen ounces before you start, sip every 15-20 minutes while you're working, and another 16 when you come in.

4. A practice — legs up the wall (2 minutes, zero effort)

Find a wall. Lie on your back with your hips close to the base. Swing your legs up so they rest flat against the wall, heels to the ceiling. Let your arms fall to your sides. Close your eyes. Two minutes.

That's the whole practice. It reverses the fluid pooling from a day on your feet, calms the nervous system through the baroreceptor response, and gives your low back a passive stretch it can't fight against. Best thing you can do after a long day of standing or yard work — and it requires exactly zero effort once you're in position.
