# Five Bullet Friday — The Longest Day Edition

*June 19*

Tomorrow is the summer solstice — the longest day of the year. You've probably been using every minute of it. Your body noticed, even if you haven't. Five things for the Friday before the sun starts its slow retreat.

---

### 1. A move — the standing calf stretch you're probably skipping

Most people stretch their hamstrings and quads. Almost nobody stretches their calves. And right now, after weeks of mowing, walking on hard ground, and standing in the heat, your calves are probably tight enough to cause problems you'd never blame them for — including that stabbing heel pain first thing in the morning.

Stand facing a wall, hands flat at shoulder height. Step one foot back about two feet, heel pressed into the floor, back leg straight. Lean forward until you feel the stretch in the upper calf. Hold 30 seconds. Now bend the back knee slightly, keeping the heel down — you'll feel the stretch shift lower, into the soleus near the achilles. Hold another 30 seconds. Switch sides.

Two minutes, both muscles, both legs. Do it after every long day on your feet.

### 2. A tool — a golf ball ($2)

You already know about the lacrosse ball and the frozen water bottle. A golf ball is the precision version — smaller, harder, and better for targeted foot work.

Stand on it barefoot (hold a counter or doorframe for balance). Roll it slowly along the arch, heel to ball of foot. When you find a spot that makes you wince, stay there. Breathe. Let the pressure do the work for 20-30 seconds, then move on. Two minutes per foot.

A golf ball gets into the small muscles between the metatarsals that a bigger ball skates over. If you're dealing with plantar fasciitis or just general foot fatigue from summer, this is the best $2 you'll spend. Check your junk drawer before you buy one — most people already have one somewhere.

### 3. A nugget — your headache might not start in your head

Janet Travell and David Simons published *Myofascial Pain and Dysfunction: The Trigger Point Manual* — it's the definitive work on how muscle knots in one place create pain in another. Their maps are still used in every serious bodywork training.

The finding that matters for you: a trigger point in the upper trapezius (that muscle between your neck and shoulder) refers pain up and over the side of the head, behind the eye. A knot in the SCM — the rope-like muscle on the front of your neck — sends pain into the forehead and behind the ear. You treat the headache with ibuprofen, but the source is 12 inches south.

If you get headaches that build through the day and ease when you lie down, the shoulders and neck are where to look. I wrote about the full chain on the blog this week.

### 4. A practice — the 2-minute body scan

This one's different from the stretches and the breathwork. It's pure awareness — no movement required.

Sit or lie down. Close your eyes. Start at the top of your head and slowly move your attention down through your body: forehead, jaw, throat, shoulders, chest, arms, hands, belly, hips, thighs, knees, calves, feet. Don't fix anything. Just notice. Where are you tight? Where are you holding? Where do you feel nothing at all?

The scan takes two minutes. What it teaches you is what your body has been trying to say all week — you've just been too busy to hear it. Most of my clients who do this regularly catch tension patterns early, before they turn into the kind of knot that needs a table visit. The longest days ask the most of your body. Checking in is the simplest thing you can do.

### 5. A seasonal note

Summer solstice weekend. The light tricks you — 15 hours of daylight makes you feel like you have 15 hours of energy. You don't. The body runs on a rhythm, not a clock, and by late June the gap between what you're doing and what you're recovering from starts to show.

This is the week I see it on the table. Someone comes in and says they feel tired for no reason. There's always a reason. Three months of ramping activity, longer days, more heat, less sleep. The longest day is a good day to take stock.

If something's been building — a shoulder that won't drop, a low back that aches every morning, feet that hurt before you even start — this is the right time to deal with it. Before the Fourth of July push, before the rest of summer stacks on top.
