# The Air-Conditioning Body Reset

*A Between Sessions bonus protocol for the summer tension pattern desk workers don't see coming.*

---

Nobody talks about this, but summer air conditioning is one of the biggest drivers of neck and shoulder tension I see. Your body can't figure out if it's July or November.

You walk out of your house into 95-degree humidity. Your muscles loosen, your tissue gets pliable, your blood flow increases. Your body adapts to the heat. Then 12 minutes later you walk into an air-conditioned office at 68 degrees. Your muscles tighten back up, your tissue stiffens, your blood flow constricts. Your body adapts to the cold.

Then you go to lunch. Heat again. Then back to the office. Cold again. Then your car for the drive home. Whatever temperature that is. Then home, which is somewhere in between. Five or six full thermal swings in a single workday.

Your muscles are constantly readjusting. They never get to settle into one state. By the end of the day they're locked up, mostly in your neck, shoulders, and upper back — the areas most exposed to direct AC airflow and most responsive to temperature shifts.

This is a separate problem from your desk posture. Desk workers in summer get hit twice — once by the posture, once by the thermal stress. The fix is different for each.

## What's actually happening in your tissue

Cold makes muscle fibers contract slightly to generate heat. Connective tissue becomes less pliable. Blood flow to skin and surface muscles decreases (your body is conserving heat for your core). When you sit at a desk in cold air for hours, the muscles in your neck, traps, and upper back are quietly locked in a low-grade contraction the whole time.

Heat does the opposite. Muscles relax. Connective tissue becomes more pliable. Blood flow increases. Your body opens up.

When you cycle between these states multiple times a day, the muscles don't get to settle into either state. They're constantly adjusting, which is its own kind of fatigue.

The areas most affected:

- **Posterior neck** — directly exposed to overhead AC vents. The muscles at the base of your skull tighten in the cold and never fully release.
- **Upper trapezius** — the meaty part between your neck and shoulder. Hunched-up shoulders are partly stress, partly cold response. You're literally trying to keep your neck warm.
- **Upper back / between shoulder blades** — exposed surface area, prone to chilling, prone to cumulative tightness.
- **Jaw** — yes, the jaw. Cold makes you clench. Watch yourself next time you walk into a cold room.

## The fix — four moves, 90 seconds total

You're not going to control your office thermostat. So we work with the body's adjustment process instead of against it.

### 1. Transition warm-up (when you walk into the cold)

Right when you enter a cold space, do this within the first 60 seconds:

- 5 shoulder rolls backward, slow
- 5 slow neck rotations each direction
- Open and close your mouth wide once, then jaw side-to-side once each direction

That tells your body "we're transitioning, prepare gradually" instead of letting the muscles brace silently in response.

### 2. Cover the neck

This isn't fashion. This is function. If your office or car runs cold:

- Wear a light scarf, bandana, or hooded sweatshirt that covers the back of your neck
- Keep it in your bag for over-AC'd meetings, restaurants, movie theaters
- The back of your neck is one of the most temperature-sensitive areas of your body — keeping it covered means your muscles stop bracing

Older clients figured this out generations ago — "don't go outside with a wet head, you'll get a stiff neck" wasn't wrong, it was practical knowledge.

### 3. Mid-day shoulder shrug release

Every couple of hours in cold environments:

- Shoulders up to ears, hold 3 seconds, drop completely
- Repeat 5 times
- Then 5 slow shoulder rolls back, big circles

This counteracts the hunched-up cold response that builds invisibly over the workday.

### 4. End-of-day hot shower or warm compress

This is the recovery move. Before bed, even just 3 minutes:

- Hot water (as warm as comfortable) directly on the back of your neck, upper traps, and between your shoulder blades
- OR a warm rice sock on those areas while you wind down
- The heat undoes the all-day cold contraction

If you only do one move from this protocol, do this one.

## Why this matters more in your 40s and beyond

Younger bodies handle thermal swings without much consequence. The tissue is more elastic, the recovery is faster, the cumulative load doesn't build as quickly.

After 40, your body's adjustment response slows. Cold takes longer to release. Heat takes longer to penetrate deep tissue. The cumulative effect of daily thermal cycling shows up as chronic upper back and neck tightness that doesn't seem to track to any specific cause.

It's not your imagination and it's not "just aging." It's a fixable pattern. You're doing four things to your body every day that you can adjust.

## The summer-specific weekly check-in

For the next week, ask yourself once a day:

- How many temperature swings did I cycle through today?
- Where in my body am I holding the most tension right now?
- Did I cover my neck in cold spaces?
- Did I do the end-of-day warm reset?

The pattern will show up fast. Once you see it, you can interrupt it.

## Closing

Keep your neck covered when you walk into the cold, move your shoulders when you walk back into the heat. Simple stuff. Huge difference.

Most of summer tension isn't about the summer activities — it's about the indoor-outdoor thermal cycling. Fix the cycle and the tension drops noticeably within a week. Cheap, simple, almost no time required.
