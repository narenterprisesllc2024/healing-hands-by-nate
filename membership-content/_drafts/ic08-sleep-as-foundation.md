## What's Actually Happening When You Sleep

Sleep isn't passive rest. It's an active, structured process your body uses to do its most important maintenance work.

### Sleep stages

Each night your body cycles through different sleep stages, each with specific functions:

**Stage 1 (light sleep) — 1-5 minutes per cycle:** Transition from wake to sleep. Brain waves slow. Easy to wake. Doesn't contribute much to restoration.

**Stage 2 (light sleep) — 10-25 minutes per cycle:** Body temperature drops, heart rate slows. Brain activity shows specific patterns (sleep spindles, K-complexes) that are involved in memory consolidation. This is roughly 45-55% of total sleep.

**Stage 3 (deep sleep / slow-wave sleep) — 20-40 minutes per cycle:** The deepest, most restorative sleep. Heart rate and breathing at lowest. Growth hormone releases for tissue repair. Glymphatic system clears metabolic waste from the brain. Critical for physical recovery. Roughly 15-25% of total sleep when healthy.

**REM sleep (rapid eye movement) — 10-60 minutes per cycle, increasing through the night:** Dream sleep. Brain activity similar to waking. Emotional processing, memory consolidation, creative integration. Roughly 20-25% of total sleep when healthy.

You cycle through these stages roughly every 90 minutes. A full night's sleep includes 4-6 complete cycles.

### Why each stage matters

If sleep gets cut short — particularly the second half of the night — you lose disproportionate amounts of REM sleep and the longer REM periods that happen near morning. This is why "I slept 6 hours instead of 8" isn't 25% less sleep; it's much greater loss of specific sleep functions.

Same problem cuts you short on deep sleep if disrupted in the first third of the night.

### What sleep accomplishes

During the various stages:

- **Tissue repair accelerates** — growth hormone peaks during deep sleep, driving muscle recovery, fascia repair, and connective tissue maintenance
- **Brain clears metabolic waste** — cerebrospinal fluid actively flushes during sleep, removing byproducts of daytime brain activity (this is why poor sleep correlates with cognitive fog)
- **Memory consolidation** — both physical (motor learning) and emotional (processing the day's events)
- **Immune function resets** — inflammatory markers reduce, immune cells multiply, the body's defenses recalibrate
- **Hormonal regulation** — cortisol cycles reset, growth hormone releases, sex hormones cycle properly
- **Nervous system shifts to parasympathetic dominance** — the only sustained window where your autonomic nervous system is fully in rest-and-repair mode
- **Glucose regulation** — insulin sensitivity is restored
- **Mood regulation** — emotional processing happens in REM; lack of REM correlates with mood disturbance

In other words: most of what you think of as "recovery" actually happens during sleep. Waking activity uses energy. Sleeping activity rebuilds. The two aren't interchangeable.

### What happens when sleep is chronically inadequate

Sustained sleep deprivation (less than 6-7 hours regularly, or fragmented sleep) is associated with:

- Increased systemic inflammation
- Impaired immune function
- Reduced cognitive performance (memory, attention, decision-making, reaction time)
- Mood disturbance (depression, anxiety, irritability)
- Insulin resistance and weight gain
- Cardiovascular stress
- Accelerated aging markers
- Reduced muscle recovery and athletic performance
- Reduced effectiveness of any other recovery interventions (including bodywork)

This isn't catastrophizing. It's well-established research. Chronic sleep deprivation is a health risk on par with smoking or sedentary lifestyle.

## Common Sleep Dysfunction Patterns

After hearing thousands of sleep complaints from clients, these are the patterns I see most often. Each has different causes and different interventions.

### Pattern 1 — Can't fall asleep (sleep onset insomnia)

**Symptom:** Lying in bed awake for 30+ minutes regularly. Mind racing. Sometimes anxiety about not sleeping makes it worse.

**Common causes:**
- Sympathetic dominance at bedtime (nervous system not downshifting)
- Caffeine too late in the day
- Screen exposure before bed (light + content effects)
- Inconsistent sleep schedule
- Anxiety / racing thoughts
- Stimulating activities or stressful conversations before bed

**What helps:**
- The pre-bed wind-down sequence from Bonus 3
- Caffeine cut-off at noon
- Screens off 60-90 minutes before bed
- Consistent bedtime
- Cool room temperature (65-68°F)
- The 4-7-8 breathing pattern (or 4-8 simpler version)
- Cognitive techniques: if you're not asleep in 20 minutes, get out of bed, do something boring with low light, return when sleepy

**When to escalate:** If insomnia persists for more than a few weeks despite addressing the basics, see your physician. May warrant evaluation for anxiety, depression, or other underlying causes.

### Pattern 2 — Wakes during the night (sleep maintenance insomnia)

**Symptom:** Falls asleep fine but wakes 1-3 times during the night. Sometimes can return to sleep, sometimes lies awake for hours.

**Common causes:**
- Stress / cortisol surges in early morning (3-4 AM waking is classic)
- Alcohol disrupts sleep architecture (you fall asleep faster but wake during the night)
- Bedroom temperature (too warm)
- Bathroom needs (especially with alcohol or large evening fluid intake)
- Sleep apnea (often unrecognized)
- Hormonal changes (menopause, perimenopause)
- Chronic pain
- Sleep partner disruption (snoring, restless movement)

**What helps:**
- Address stress factors during the day
- Reduce or eliminate alcohol (especially within 3 hours of bedtime)
- Cool room
- Limit fluids in the 2 hours before bed
- If hormonal — talk to your physician about options
- If pain — bedwork, bedding changes, possibly medication
- If sleep apnea suspected — sleep study (this is important)

### Pattern 3 — Wakes too early (early morning waking)

**Symptom:** Wakes at 3-5 AM regularly and can't return to sleep. Sometimes feels "wired."

**Common causes:**
- Anxiety or depression (early morning waking is a classic depression symptom)
- Cortisol dysregulation
- Alcohol (you fall asleep fast but it disrupts sleep architecture)
- Inadequate sleep total earlier in the cycle (your body just woke up "early" because it caught up on the deficit)
- Aging (some shift toward earlier wake is normal with age)

**What helps:**
- Address the underlying cause (this is where the basics often aren't enough alone)
- Reduce alcohol
- Address sleep hygiene
- If anxiety/depression — mental health support is often needed
- Some people benefit from melatonin in the middle of the night (talk to your doctor)

### Pattern 4 — Sleeps but not refreshed (poor sleep quality)

**Symptom:** Gets adequate sleep hours but wakes feeling unrefreshed. Tired all day despite reasonable sleep time.

**Common causes:**
- Sleep apnea — major cause, often unrecognized
- Restless leg syndrome / periodic limb movement disorder
- Poor sleep architecture (lots of light sleep, not enough deep or REM)
- Alcohol use
- Sleep medication use (some medications give you sleep that isn't restorative)
- Sleep environment (bedroom too noisy, light, warm, etc.)
- Bedding issues (wrong mattress, wrong pillow)

**What helps:**
- Sleep study to rule out apnea (this is often where the answer is)
- Address sleep environment
- Reduce alcohol
- Talk to physician about medication choices
- Bedding adjustments per Module 3

**When to escalate:** Persistent unrefreshing sleep despite adequate hours warrants medical evaluation. Sleep apnea is treatable but needs diagnosis.

### Pattern 5 — Variable / inconsistent sleep

**Symptom:** Some weeks good, some weeks bad. Hard to predict. Sleep quality bounces around.

**Common causes:**
- Inconsistent schedule (different bedtime/wake time across days)
- Variable evening routines
- Stress that comes and goes
- Hormonal fluctuations (especially in women)
- Travel
- Shift work

**What helps:**
- Establish more consistent schedule (within a 30-minute window if possible)
- Standardize evening routine
- Identify which factors correlate with bad sleep nights
- Address whatever's variable in your life

### Pattern 6 — "I've always been a bad sleeper"

**Symptom:** Lifelong pattern of poor sleep. Believes they "just don't need as much sleep" or are "wired for less sleep."

**Reality:** The "short sleepers" who genuinely need only 4-6 hours are extremely rare (genuine genetic variation, less than 1% of population). Almost everyone who thinks they're a short sleeper is actually chronically sleep-deprived but has normalized it.

**What helps:**
- Honest assessment: are you actually thriving on short sleep, or have you adapted to dysfunction?
- If you've never really tried to sleep adequately — try a 30-day experiment of 8 hours nightly with proper sleep hygiene. Notice what changes.
- Often there's underlying chronic dysregulation that needs addressed — sometimes a sleep specialist consultation

## Sleep Hygiene Deep Dive

Sleep hygiene is the set of practices that support good sleep. The basics are well-known. Most people don't actually do all of them consistently.

### Environmental factors

**Temperature:** 65-68°F is optimal for most adults. Body needs a slight core temperature drop to initiate and maintain deep sleep. Too warm fragments sleep stages.

**Light:** Pitch dark is ideal. Any light source — phone screens, alarm clocks, light leaking around curtains — affects melatonin and sleep depth. Blackout curtains are worth it if your bedroom has external light. Eye masks are a low-cost alternative.

**Sound:** Consistent is better than variable. White noise, fan noise, brown noise all work — the consistency masks variable noises that would otherwise wake you. Earplugs work for some people.

**Bedding:** Mattress should support your spine in a neutral position (medium-firm for most adults). Pillow appropriate to your sleep position (thicker for side sleepers, thinner for back sleepers, no pillow for stomach sleepers — though you should ideally stop being a stomach sleeper).

**Bedroom use:** Ideally bedroom is for sleep and sex only. Working in bed, watching TV in bed, doomscrolling in bed — all train your brain to associate the bed with wakefulness.

### Behavioral factors

**Caffeine:** Cut off by noon for most adults. Caffeine half-life is 5-7 hours. Caffeine at 2 PM is still half-active at 8 PM. Even people who "can sleep fine with caffeine" usually have lighter, less restorative sleep.

**Alcohol:** Reduces sleep quality significantly even when it helps you fall asleep faster. Alcohol disrupts REM sleep and causes early-morning waking. For sleep optimization, ideally no alcohol within 3 hours of bedtime. For best sleep, less or no alcohol overall.

**Screens:** Off 60-90 minutes before bed if possible. The blue light suppresses melatonin. The content keeps your nervous system engaged. The combination interferes significantly with sleep onset and depth.

**Food:** Heavy meals within 2-3 hours of bedtime interfere with sleep. Light snack is fine. Some people sleep better with a small carb-and-protein snack before bed; others sleep better completely empty. Experiment with what works for you.

**Exercise:** Helps sleep overall but not within 1-2 hours of bedtime for most people (raises body temperature and stimulates nervous system). Morning or afternoon exercise is best for sleep support.

**Schedule consistency:** Going to bed and waking up within a 30-minute window every day (including weekends) outperforms varying your schedule. Your circadian rhythm thrives on consistency.

### Pre-sleep routine

A consistent pre-sleep routine signals your nervous system that sleep is coming. Use Bonus 3 (Pre-Bed Wind-Down Sequence) as the template. The key elements:

- Dim lights 60-90 minutes before bed
- Cool room
- Transition activities (reading paper book, gentle stretching, breathing)
- Skip screens
- Wind down nervous system through specific practices (legs up wall, progressive muscle relaxation, diaphragmatic breathing)

The routine is more important than any single element. Consistency of the routine itself signals "sleep is coming" to your brain.

## Sleep and Aging

Sleep patterns change with age. Some changes are real biology. Others are accumulated dysfunction.

### What changes naturally

- Sleep architecture shifts toward less deep sleep and more light sleep
- Total sleep need decreases slightly (though not as much as people assume — older adults still need 7-8 hours)
- Sleep timing tends to shift earlier (earlier bedtime, earlier wake)
- Sleep becomes more fragmented (more brief awakenings)

### What's NOT inevitable

- Severely fragmented sleep
- Insomnia
- Daytime fatigue
- Loss of significant sleep quality

Most "elderly people don't sleep well" stories are actually treatable sleep disorders that were never properly addressed. Sleep apnea is dramatically more common in older adults and dramatically undiagnosed.

### Special considerations after 50

- Sleep medications carry higher risks (interaction with other medications, fall risk, cognitive effects)
- Hormonal changes in menopause significantly affect sleep — work with your physician
- Bladder issues becoming more common — limit evening fluids
- Joint pain may need to be addressed (bedwork, possibly pain management)
- Sleep apnea risk increases — get evaluated if you snore loudly, wake feeling unrefreshed, or your partner notices you stop breathing

## When to Get a Sleep Study

A sleep study is a medical evaluation of your sleep. Either done in a sleep lab (overnight) or at home with portable equipment.

**Consider a sleep study if:**

- You snore loudly (especially if your partner has noticed you stop breathing or gasp)
- You wake up unrefreshed despite adequate sleep hours
- Daytime fatigue significantly impairs function
- You've gained weight and sleep quality has decreased
- High blood pressure that isn't responding to typical interventions
- Morning headaches
- Diagnosed with conditions linked to sleep apnea (heart disease, type 2 diabetes, AFib)
- Severe insomnia that hasn't responded to sleep hygiene improvements

Sleep apnea is treatable. CPAP and other interventions work well. But you need diagnosis first.

Other conditions that sleep studies diagnose:
- Restless leg syndrome
- Periodic limb movement disorder
- Narcolepsy
- REM sleep behavior disorder
- Parasomnias

Many of these are treatable. Many people suffer for years before getting diagnosed.

## Common Sleep Medications and Supplements

I'm not your physician, so this isn't medical advice. This is the honest information I share with clients when they ask.

### Melatonin

**What it is:** A hormone your body produces naturally that regulates the sleep-wake cycle.

**Use cases:** Some help for jet lag, shift work, sleep onset insomnia, age-related melatonin decline.

**Honest take:** Best for circadian rhythm issues, not generic insomnia. Most effective at low doses (0.3-1mg) taken 30-60 minutes before bed. Higher doses (5-10mg sold OTC) often work less well and have more side effects (vivid dreams, morning grogginess).

**Risks:** Generally safe short-term. Long-term effects less studied. Talk to physician if using nightly long-term.

### Magnesium

**What it is:** Mineral involved in muscle relaxation and nervous system regulation.

**Use cases:** Some help for sleep, anxiety, muscle cramps. Better-studied than many supplements.

**Honest take:** Magnesium glycinate or magnesium threonate seem to work best for sleep. Magnesium citrate also works for some people. Avoid magnesium oxide (poor absorption, GI effects).

**Risks:** Generally safe at recommended doses. Too much causes GI issues. Talk to physician if on certain medications (some interact).

### Prescription sleep medications

**Categories:** Benzodiazepines (Ativan, Xanax), Z-drugs (Ambien, Lunesta), antihistamines (Trazodone, Doxepin), others.

**Honest take:** These have a place for short-term use or specific situations. They are not great long-term solutions because: they don't produce normal sleep architecture (you fall asleep but sleep is less restorative), tolerance develops, some have significant side effects, some cause dependence.

If you're on long-term prescription sleep medication, talk with your physician about whether it's still appropriate and whether other approaches have been tried adequately.

### Things that probably don't help much

- Most "sleep formula" supplements
- CBD for sleep (mixed evidence)
- Most herbal blends (mixed to poor evidence)

These aren't dangerous — they're just usually not very effective despite marketing claims.

## How Sleep Affects the Body Work I Do

This is the practical takeaway for HHBN members specifically.

### When sleep is good

- Bodywork releases hold longer
- Tissue is more responsive during sessions
- Nervous system shifts more easily into parasympathetic during work
- You feel "lighter" after sessions and the lightness lasts
- Daily home practice is more effective
- Pain thresholds are normal

### When sleep is poor

- Bodywork results don't last
- Tissue is more reactive and harder to release
- Nervous system stays sympathetic during sessions
- Acute pain doesn't resolve well
- Daily practice has less impact
- Pain thresholds are lower (everything hurts more)

This is why I keep emphasizing sleep. The work I do has a much higher ceiling when your sleep is good. It has a much lower ceiling when your sleep is poor.

Clients who fix their sleep get more out of fewer sessions. Clients who don't end up needing more sessions because the work doesn't hold.

If you're investing in bodywork — with me or anyone else — the highest-leverage thing you can do to maximize that investment is invest in your sleep.

## What to Expect in a Year of Consistent Sleep Optimization

**Week 1-2:** Establishing the routine. Some immediate improvements from environmental changes (cooler room, no screens).

**Month 1:** Schedule consistency starts paying off. Falling asleep faster. Fewer night wakings.

**Month 2-3:** Sleep architecture improves. Waking more refreshed. Daytime energy more consistent.

**Month 4-6:** Cumulative effects compound. Mood, cognitive function, physical recovery all noticeably improved. Bodywork sessions producing better results.

**Month 7-12:** Sleep becomes a non-issue. You sleep well most nights. When you have an occasional bad night, you know why and it doesn't derail you. The foundation is solid.

This is realistic for someone addressing sleep systematically. If you have an undiagnosed sleep disorder, you may need medical intervention before lifestyle changes are effective. Don't dismiss the possibility.

## Closing

Sleep is the foundation. Not metaphorically — actually, biologically, the foundation that other recovery practices build on.

This document is your reference for getting that foundation right. Most people who address sleep systematically experience significant improvements within weeks to months. Many discover that issues they thought were unrelated (mood, pain, recovery, cognitive function) were actually downstream of inadequate sleep.

If your sleep is currently working well, maintain it. The practices that produce good sleep are also the practices that prevent the slow drift into poor sleep that affects most adults over time.

If your sleep isn't working — start with the basics. Cool room. Consistent schedule. Caffeine cut-off. Screens off. The pre-bed wind-down. If those don't work after a few weeks, escalate to a physician or sleep specialist. Don't accept poor sleep as inevitable.
