# Sleep & Recovery Basics

*A between-sessions module on the part of recovery that happens while you're not awake — and why it's the foundation of everything else.*

---

I can do the best bodywork session of my career on you, and if you go home and sleep four hours on a bad pillow, we're starting over next week.

I'm telling you this honestly because it's the most consistent pattern I see. The clients whose bodywork actually holds are the ones who sleep well. The clients whose bodywork comes undone in three days are almost always the ones who don't.

It's not about effort on the table. It's not about technique. It's about whether the body actually gets the recovery window it needs to integrate the work.

Sleep is that window. Without it, nothing else in your recovery chain works as well as it could.

This module is about what sleep actually does for your body, the three things that wreck it most, and the simple wind-down routine that gets you most of the way there without buying anything.

## What sleep actually does for your body

This is the part most people underestimate. Sleep isn't just "rest." It's the active maintenance window your body needs to do its real repair work.

During deep sleep stages specifically:

- **Tissue repair accelerates.** Growth hormone releases at peak levels, driving muscle recovery, fascia repair, and connective tissue maintenance.
- **The brain clears metabolic waste.** Cerebrospinal fluid actively flushes the brain during sleep, removing byproducts that accumulated during waking hours. This is why poor sleep correlates with brain fog and mood issues.
- **Memory consolidation happens.** Both physical motor memory and emotional processing happen during specific sleep stages. Skill acquisition, including movement skills, depends on sleep.
- **Immune function resets.** Inflammatory markers reduce, immune cells multiply, the body's defense systems recalibrate.
- **Nervous system shifts to parasympathetic dominance.** This is the only sustained window in your day when your autonomic nervous system is fully in rest-and-repair mode.

In other words: most of the actual recovery you think happens "in general" actually happens during sleep specifically. Your waking body uses energy. Your sleeping body rebuilds. The two are not interchangeable.

A 4-hour sleep night doesn't give you 4 hours of recovery. It gives you significantly less because the deep recovery stages — which only show up in the second half of a full night's sleep — barely happen at all in short sleeps.

This isn't about being lazy. It's about giving your body the window it needs to do its actual job.

## The three things that wreck sleep most

After 12+ years on the table, hearing thousands of sleep complaints, three causes account for 80% of the disrupted sleep I encounter:

### 1. Screens before bed

The light from screens (phones, tablets, TVs, computers) suppresses melatonin production. Specifically, the blue-spectrum light tells your circadian system "it's still daytime." Even 30 minutes of screen exposure within an hour of bed delays melatonin release.

This is true even if you have "night mode" or blue-light filters on. Those help somewhat but don't eliminate the effect.

It's also not just the light. The content matters too. Doomscrolling activates your stress response. Watching action-packed shows activates your sympathetic nervous system. Reading work emails primes your brain for problem-solving mode. None of those are sleep-promoting states.

The 60-90 minutes before bed should ideally be screen-free, or at minimum, dramatically lower-stimulation.

### 2. Caffeine after noon

Caffeine has a half-life of 5-7 hours in most adults. That means caffeine you drink at 2 PM is still half-active in your system at 8 PM, and a quarter-active at midnight.

You might be the person who says "caffeine doesn't affect my sleep." You might be right, in that you can fall asleep. But caffeine reduces deep sleep quality even when it doesn't prevent sleep onset. The sleep you get is lighter, more disrupted, less restorative.

The cut-off depends on your sensitivity. For most adults, no caffeine after noon is the safest rule. For caffeine-sensitive people, the cut-off should be earlier.

This includes coffee, black tea, green tea, energy drinks, and many sodas. Pre-workout supplements often have very high doses.

### 3. Inconsistent schedule

Your circadian rhythm thrives on consistency. Going to bed at 10 PM Monday, midnight Tuesday, 11 PM Wednesday, and 1 AM Friday is jet lag your body experiences every week.

The body wants to wake up at the same time every day, including weekends. The wake time is more important than the bedtime — your wake time anchors the next night's sleep timing.

People who sleep 8 hours a night with a consistent schedule almost always feel more rested than people who sleep 8.5 hours with a variable schedule. The total hours matter, but the consistency matters more.

## Temperature matters more than people realize

This is the underappreciated factor. Your body needs a slight core temperature drop to initiate and maintain deep sleep.

The ideal sleep environment for most adults:

- **Room temperature: 65-68°F.** Cooler than most people keep their houses during the day.
- **Warm blankets.** The combination of cool air + warm covers is what your body responds to best.
- **Avoid being too warm.** Overheating during sleep fragments your sleep stages and increases night waking.

If you find yourself kicking covers off in the middle of the night, your room is too warm. If you're shivering, it's too cold. The sweet spot is "I want a blanket but I'm not sweating under it."

## A simple wind-down routine that doesn't require buying anything

This is the practical part. Here's the routine I tell every client I work with:

### The hour before bed

The hour before your target sleep time is your wind-down hour. The goal of this hour is to actively signal to your nervous system that it's time to transition from doing to resting.

**Minute 0-15: Dim the lights.** Lower the brightness in whatever room you're in. Turn off overhead lights. Use lamps. Use bedside lights. The lower light level signals melatonin production to begin.

**Minute 15-30: Last screen window.** If you must use screens, do it now. Then put them away. After this point, no more screens.

**Minute 30-45: Pre-bed wind-down.** This is where you actively shift the body. Use the wind-down sequence from B3 if you have it. Or just sit quietly with low lights, maybe read a paper book, maybe do gentle stretching.

**Minute 45-60: Sleep prep.** Brush teeth, get into bed. Use 5 minutes of slow diaphragmatic breathing once you're horizontal to finalize the parasympathetic shift.

That's it. No supplements required. No equipment required. Just an hour of deliberate transition.

If you can't carve out a full hour, even 30 minutes of this routine improves sleep noticeably.

## The pillow and mattress question

Most people are sleeping on bedding that's wrong for their body. Two quick checks:

**Pillow check:** When you're lying on your side, your head should be in line with your spine. If your head tips down (pillow too thin) or up (pillow too thick), the pillow's wrong. Side sleepers need thicker pillows. Back sleepers need thinner pillows.

**Mattress check:** When you lie flat on your back, slide your hand under your low back. If it slips through easily and you can wiggle it, the mattress is too firm. If you can't fit your hand at all, it's too soft. The right firmness is "I can slide my hand in but it's snug."

Bad bedding is a low-grade, all-night source of strain. Most chronic morning pain that doesn't have another explanation traces back to one of these two factors.

You don't have to buy a new mattress (often). You can add a memory foam topper for less. You can change pillows for under $50. The investment pays back in actual sleep quality.

## Sleep position

The order, from best to worst for spinal alignment:

1. **Side sleeping with pillow between knees.** Best for most adults. Pillow between knees keeps hips aligned.
2. **Back sleeping with pillow under knees.** Good if you don't have sleep apnea. Pillow under knees protects low back.
3. **Side sleeping without pillow between knees.** Decent, but creates some hip torque.
4. **Back sleeping flat.** Decent, but can stress low back.
5. **Stomach sleeping.** Worst. Forces neck rotation, arches low back all night.

If you sleep on your stomach, it's the position most worth changing if you have chronic neck or low back pain. Most people who switch report better sleep within two weeks.

## What to expect

Two weeks of fixing the basics (cool room, screen cut-off, consistent schedule, pillow check):

- Falling asleep faster
- Fewer night wakings
- Waking up actually feeling rested
- Less morning stiffness
- More energy through the afternoon (when sleep debt usually shows up as the 3 PM crash)

The improvements are real and noticeable within two weeks. Three months of consistent good sleep and most people don't recognize how much better they feel than they did before.

## When sleep problems aren't lifestyle problems

If you've corrected all of the above for 4-6 weeks and your sleep is still bad, it's time to talk to a physician. There are real sleep disorders — sleep apnea, restless leg syndrome, periodic limb movement disorder — that require medical intervention. They don't respond to lifestyle changes alone.

A sleep study can identify these. If you wake up tired no matter how many hours you sleep, snore loudly, or your bed partner notices you stop breathing during sleep, ask your doctor about a sleep study.

This module is about lifestyle factors. It can't fix a clinical sleep disorder.
